\section{Conclusion} \label{conclu}

We revisited Reversible Instance Normalization to clarify which of its components improve time-series forecasting and which distribution shifts it can address. Across three real-world datasets and a controlled synthetic dataset, instance normalization generally improved generalization to future periods and unseen series. However, these gains were not universal: standard normalization performed best on \textsc{Traffic}, showing that removing instance-level scale and offset can be detrimental when these statistics are informative and the initial distribution discrepancy is limited.

Our ablations further showed that RevIN's learnable symmetric affine transformation provides negligible benefit, whereas computing the training loss in normalized space consistently improves performance. This suggests that the main advantages of RevIN arise from aligning instance-level input statistics and balancing the contribution of differently scaled series during optimization.

Our distributional analysis also qualifies the notion of alignment provided by instance normalization. Although it eliminates differences in first and second order statistics, it does not necessarily align higher-order structure, frequency content, or temporal dynamics. Moreover, RevIN assumes that the normalized relationship between look-back and horizon statistics can be learned as an approximately invariant shift. Our synthetic experiments show that an asymmetric, context-dependent denormalization can explicitly represent such shifts and outperform RevIN when this assumption holds. In real-world data, however, these shifts vary across instances and time.

Overall, instance normalization should be viewed as a useful but lossy inductive bias rather than a general solution to distribution shift. Future normalization methods should preserve or selectively reintroduce predictive information from the original input statistics while adapting the denormalization mapping to the series and temporal context.
